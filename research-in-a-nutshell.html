Question Framework

Build your research question, PICO or SPIDER statement, and hypothesis.

Not every study uses PICO. PICO is built for intervention and comparative questions — it works well when you have an identifiable treatment, a comparison condition, and a measurable outcome. When those elements are absent, PICO produces forced or meaningless entries. Use the comparison table below to pick the right framework for your study.
Study typeProblem with PICOBetter fit
Intervention / comparative—PICO ✓
Qualitative / exploratoryNo intervention, no comparison, no measurable outcomeSPIDER
Observational / etiologicalNo intervention — studying exposure or riskPECO or direct RQ
Descriptive / normativeNo intervention or comparisonDirect RQ
LongitudinalTime is a key variable not capturedPICOT (adds T = Time)
Mixed methodsOnly addresses one strandPICO + SPIDER (one per strand)
◆ PICO / PECO — Intervention, Comparative & Observational Studies
PICO and PECO share three elements. Population, Comparison, and Outcome are defined identically in both — the only difference is the second element: Intervention (PICO) vs. Exposure (PECO). Use Intervention when you're testing an assigned treatment; use Exposure when you're studying a risk factor, condition, or naturally occurring variable with no assigned treatment.

◆ SPIDER — Qualitative & Exploratory Studies
SPIDER (Cooke, Smith & Booth, 2012) was developed specifically for qualitative and mixed-methods systematic reviews. It replaces intervention-focused elements with ones suited to exploratory research. Note: SPIDER is more commonly used in systematic reviews than in individual studies — a direct, well-framed research question is also acceptable for standalone qualitative work.

◇ Single-Subject Experimental Designs (SSEDs)
SSEDs are true experimental designs — they require IV manipulation and demonstrate functional relationships through replication. Reliability & Validity

Two distinct but related measurement properties. Learn the concepts, then select the types relevant to your study.

Reliability asks: Does this measure produce consistent results?

Validity asks: Does this measure actually assess what it claims to?

The critical relationship: Reliability is a prerequisite for validity — but a measure can be perfectly reliable while being completely invalid.

Sampling

Learn the sampling strategies and their tradeoffs, then select the approach for your study.

Probability vs. non-probability: Probability sampling uses randomness so every population member has a known selection chance — the foundation for statistical generalization. Non-probability sampling is far more common in clinical CSD research and is entirely legitimate. Be transparent about who your sample represents and does not represent.

How many participants? Detecting a medium effect with two independent groups typically requires 26–64 per group; paired designs require fewer. Run the Power Analysis calculator for a precise figure.

Inclusion vs. exclusion criteria are not the same as population definition. Inclusion defines who qualifies; exclusion defines reasons to screen someone out even if they meet inclusion criteria. Both must be explicitly stated.
◆ Probability Sampling — Strategy Definitions
Simple Random Sampling Probability
Every individual in the population has an equal, independent chance of being selected. Eliminates selection bias and provides the strongest basis for generalization — but requires a complete, accessible population list, which is rarely available in clinical CSD settings.
Pros
Minimizes selection bias; strongest generalizability
CSD Example
Randomly selecting adults with aphasia from a complete hospital database
Stratified Random Sampling Probability
The population is divided into subgroups (strata) based on a relevant characteristic, then random samples are drawn from each stratum separately. Guarantees proportional representation of key subgroups.
Pros
Ensures representation of key subgroups even if small
CSD Example
Stratifying SLPs by work setting then randomly sampling from each stratum
Systematic Sampling Probability
Every nth person is selected from an ordered list. Simpler to execute than simple random sampling and nearly as effective when the list has no cyclical pattern.
CSD Example
Selecting every 5th patient from a clinic intake log over 12 months
Caution
Cyclical patterns in the list can introduce bias
Cluster Sampling Probability
Natural groups (clusters) are randomly selected, then individuals within those clusters are recruited. Practical when group-level lists exist but individual population lists do not.
CSD Example
Randomly selecting 10 school districts, then recruiting all children with IEP communication goals from those districts
Caution
Within-cluster similarity can reduce effective sample size
◇ Non-Probability Sampling — Strategy Definitions
Convenience Sampling Non-Probability
Participants are selected based on easy access and availability. The most common approach in clinical CSD research — legitimate for exploratory and clinical work, but requires explicit acknowledgment of limitations in the discussion section.
CSD Example
Recruiting from your own clinic caseload or a university speech clinic
Limitation
Participants may not represent the broader clinical population
Purposive Sampling Non-Probability
Participants are intentionally selected because they meet specific, predefined characteristics directly relevant to the research question. Also called criterion-based or judgmental sampling.
CSD Example
Recruiting only monolingual English-speaking adults with post-stroke left-hemisphere aphasia, 6–24 months post-onset
Best for
When participant characteristics are essential to the research question
Snowball Sampling Non-Probability
Existing participants recruit additional participants from their personal networks. Most useful for hard-to-reach or rare populations where no accessible recruitment list exists.
CSD Example
Recruiting adults with rare progressive aphasias through referrals from initial participants
Limitation
High risk of network bias; sample may overrepresent connected subgroups
Quota Sampling Non-Probability
A fixed number of participants are recruited from predefined categories to ensure balanced group representation. Similar to stratified sampling but without random selection within categories.
CSD Example
Recruiting 20 children with autism and 20 typically developing children for a social communication comparison
Caution
Non-random selection within quotas can introduce selection bias
Theoretical Sampling Non-Probability
Sampling decisions are guided by emerging themes as data collection progresses. Unique to qualitative research — sampling continues until theoretical saturation, not based on a predetermined number.
CSD Example
Beginning with school SLP interviews, then specifically seeking SLPs with large AAC caseloads as technology access themes emerge
Best for
Grounded theory and qualitative traditions requiring saturation

Ethics & Compliance

IRB & Research Ethics

Learn the ethical principles and review categories, then use the decision tree to identify your review type.

The Belmont Report (1979) established three core principles: respect for persons (autonomy and informed consent), beneficence (maximize benefit, minimize harm), and justice (equitable distribution of research burdens and benefits). All IRB requirements flow from these three principles.
Answer the questions below to identify the appropriate UCF IRB review category for your study.
Step 1
Does your study involve human participants?
Human subjects research = a systematic investigation involving living humans from whom identifiable data are obtained.
Step 2
Does your study fall into an exemption category?
Exemptions include: normal educational practices, surveys/interviews with no identifiable risk, benign behavioral interventions with adults, secondary research using de-identified existing data.
Step 3
Does your study involve greater than minimal risk to participants?
Greater than minimal risk: significant discomfort, deception, sensitive topics, invasive procedures, vulnerable populations, or identifiable data with risk of harm if disclosed.
Not Human Subjects Research
Protocol: HRP-250
May not require full IRB review. Submit HRP-250 for a determination letter confirming this status.
Exempt Review
Protocol: HRP-255 · Consent: HRP-254
Exempt studies still require IRB submission. Most survey, interview, and standard educational CSD research qualifies.
Expedited Review
Protocol: HRP-503 · Consent: HRP-502
Applies to low-risk studies that do not qualify for exemption. Most clinical CSD research with standardized assessments qualifies.
Full Board Review
Protocol: HRP-503 · Consent: HRP-502
Required when the study poses greater than minimal risk. Reviewed at a convened IRB meeting. Allow additional time for scheduling.

Effect Size

Understand what effect sizes mean, then select the measure(s) you will report.

◆ How overlap changes with effect size
Effect Size (Cohen's d) — Small, Medium, and Large
Effect size shows how much the group distributions overlap. Larger d = less overlap = more meaningful difference.
Statistical significance ≠ practical significance. A p-value tells you whether an effect is likely real. Effect size tells you whether it is large enough to matter clinically. APA 7th ed. requires effect sizes alongside all inferential statistics. Always report both.

Power Analysis & Sample Size

Understand power and error tradeoffs, then calculate minimum sample size for your design.

Statistical power is the probability your study will detect a real effect if one exists. Power = 1 − β. Most CSD research targets power = .80 — an 80% chance of detecting a real medium effect with α = .05.

About This Tool

Project notes, intent, references, and disclaimers.

Purpose & Intent

Research in a Nutshell is an interactive study guide and proposal planning tool developed for SPA 4803: Research Methods in Communication Sciences and Disorders at the University of Central Florida. It supports students as both a reference resource and a guided workspace for developing a research proposal prospectus.

This tool is not a substitute for the course textbook, faculty instruction, or independent scholarly engagement. It is intended to complement those resources — not replace them.

Study Mode & Builder Mode

Study Mode provides conceptual reference content. Builder Mode shows interactive tools. Blueprint is a dedicated tab for assembling your proposal. Selections made in any mode are preserved and shared across all views.
